01

You keep doing the same thing. You just keep getting more specific about it.

Your dad the lawyer who writes the family Gazette before dawn and drives the speed limit. Your mom the artist who swears a lot and hates airports. You grew up speaking both registers and translating between them without thinking about it.

At Conde Nast you walked into rooms where editorial, sales, events, and promotions all wanted different things, and you could see how the pieces fit. A decade of that. Then the Wing - three years, $20 million in partnerships, Chanel, SmartWater, American Express. You took $2K inbound requests and turned them into quarter-million-dollar deals. The most incredible professional journey of your life. Then it collapsed.

You didn't go back to corporate. Partnered with Wendy, 50/50. Switched lanes from sponsorship sales to partnerships as strategy. Built the Searchlight Sprint. Forager liked the strategy so much they bought it and hired you for a year. And now you're building Matchmaker - teaching yourself to code, dealing with security bugs at midnight.

They called it quarterbacking at Conde Nast. The Wing was where it turned into real money. The Sprint made it a methodology. Matchmaker is making it infrastructure. Same instinct, each time with more clarity about what it actually is.
02 - Your positioning
Cultural partnership strategist
You help brands figure out where they belong in culture and build the partnerships that get them there. And then you stay and build it with them.
03 - The vehicles

You sit above all of these. They're how the work gets delivered.

Now

Searchlight Sprint - the methodology, four weeks
Matchmaker - the platform, soft launch

How they connect

Consulting proves the model. Platform scales what consulting proves. Every sprint you run is data for Matchmaker. They compound.

04 - Why you

This isn't a career anyone would design. That's the point.

You've sat on every side of the partnership table

Media (Conde Nast, ten years). Community (Wing, three years). Creator (you got Patrick Janelle CFDA's first influencer of the year and Land Rover deals before anyone had a playbook for that). Brand (consulting). Platform (Matchmaker). You speak all of these languages because you've worked inside all of them.

You have taste, and it shows up in the work

"Agora storytelling" for an olive oil brand. "It's cool to be not cool" for quiet luxury. Ceramicists and craft markets for a yogurt company. The Women's Health panel at The Well - Perelel, Aramore, Hanni, Ketone-IQ, Love Wellness, Foria. Every one of those was curated, specific, and intentional.

Twenty years of trust

You connected Sara Auster to Hoda Kotb's Joy 101 through a relationship that started when you were an NBC Page in 2001. You get into new rooms fast, and you've kept every relationship alive across media, community, brand, creator, and tech for twenty years. That's a living rolodex.

You research deeper than anyone asks you to

EU olive oil fraud statistics. The cultural logic of "everyone wants to be a chef at home." Fifteen years of undiagnosed ADHD and dyslexia built that rigor. The diagnosis changed how hard you have to work for it. It didn't change the habit.

You get trusted fast

You put the hard stuff on the table early. Patrick Janelle chose you over a professional agent because "you understand the nuances of me and of things." You get to real conversation faster than most people get to a pitch deck.

05 - What you see that the market doesn't

Ideas that came out of your work - and what they actually mean for positioning.

"It's cool to be not cool"

Your phrase, for a quiet luxury brand going up against flashy competitors. Whether it's Chanel or a ceramicist, you find where the brand actually belongs - the specific, real entry points that connect because they're true.

"Cultural territories where they have a right to play"

Your language, from the Sprint. "Agora storytelling" for olive oil. EU fraud statistics as a data point. You think about where a brand belongs at a level most people skip.

You named the company after how you think

Ask/Offer. Every partnership has two sides. Most people only think about theirs. You named the company after holding both.

Forager bought the year, not the deck

They liked the strategy so much they hired you. You did the first 20% and then stayed to build the rest of it with them.

06 - The moment

Why the market needs this right now.

No infrastructurePartner discovery still runs on warm intros and LinkedIn DMs.
"Be in culture"CMOs told to show up in culture. Nobody's showing them where.
Agencies leaveThey deliver the deck and move on. The money dies in the gap between strategy and execution.
IRL is backBrands need physical, community-based moments. Someone has to design them.

The problems you keep hearing:

Brands that know they should be doing partnerships but don't know where to start.
Agencies that delivered a strategy deck - now sitting in a Google Drive folder.
Brands trying to show up in culture by buying their way in instead of finding where they actually belong.
07 - Where this goes

The Sprint can be taught. Matchmaker can be the infrastructure.

The Searchlight Sprint is already repeatable. Right now you and Wendy run every engagement. But it's a methodology, and methodologies can be taught, licensed, embedded inside organizations. Matchmaker is the other path - partner discovery at scale.

Both build from the same twenty years. The direction is clear. Which path gets the most energy doesn't need to be decided yet.
